Spec-driven development deserves a better spec.

Enterprise-grade software, without the enterprise overhead.

AI made building fast. It didn't make it enterprise-ready — that still takes domain modelling, the right decisions, threat models, real tests, and a paper trail you can stand behind. The Method puts the full discipline in the hands of the people building it: a small, senior team now does the work that used to need an architecture board, a security function, and a room of analysts — domain modelling, threat modelling, refinement — and does it themselves, at pace. Big-org rigour, without the moving parts.

And what you walk away with isn't only the software — it's every decision, documented and remembered: an asset that compounds for years, and a shared source of truth that keeps your whole team, and its AI agents, aligned as you grow.

New to spec-driven development? It's the fast-growing practice of letting a written spec — not the prompt — be the source of truth your AI agents build from (see GitHub's Spec Kit or AWS Kiro). Those generate a spec. The Method makes it one you'd stake production on.

Why I built this

Hello — I'm Nicky. I've spent years delivering software into enterprises: the kind of work where a missed decision, a thin threat model, or an undocumented assumption costs real money and real trust. The rigour that gets you through that — domain-driven design, event storming, disciplined refinement, threat modelling — works. It's just always needed a team of specialists and more time than anyone has.

AI changes the maths. For the first time that expertise can be on tap — patient, consistent, available to a small, senior team building at pace. So I built the Method to bring everything I'd learned shipping enterprise software into a single guided conversation. The first time I ran it on a real project it genuinely felt like magic: it asked the questions a senior team would, walked me from a rough idea to epics, tickets, and specs without me once wondering what came next, and handed me a set of documented decisions I'll still be grateful for in two years. That's what I want it to do for you.

— Nicky

One guided conversation, start to finish

From the install to your first epic, the Method runs as one continuous, guided conversation. It asks; you answer in plain language; it drafts; you sign off; it moves to the next thing — and you always know what that is. No blank page, no methodology to learn, no wondering whether you've covered everything. It's the confidence of having a senior architect, a security lead, and a business analyst in the room — minus the meetings, the hand-offs, and the two-week wait for a review slot.

Under that fluency is decades of practice most teams never get to apply: domain-driven design to find the real boundaries, event storming to map what actually happens, threat modelling that doubles as compliance evidence, and disciplined refinement that ends in tickets a developer can build without a single follow-up question. You don't have to know any of it by name. You just answer the questions.

The questions you didn't know to ask

The interview is the product. It doesn't take dictation — it pushes back, the way a staff engineer reviewing your plan would. And the things that surface are exactly the ones that cost you later if they don't surface now:

edge case

Found in the conversation, not the incident

The rejected transfer, the empty export, the two writes that race. The "what happens when…" nobody wrote down — caught before it's a 2 a.m. page.

security

Modelled before it's a pentest finding

The cross-tenant leak, the replayable token, the authorization check that isn't there. Threat-modelled at design time, while it's still cheap to close.

decision

Captured the moment it's made

The choice a new hire will ask "why?" about in six months — written down as an ADR, with the reasoning and the alternatives, not lost to a Slack thread.

scope

The feature you hadn't thought of

Obvious once the right question is asked — and far cheaper to design now than to retrofit after launch.

The assets outlast the software

Code gets refactored, rewritten, replaced. The thinking behind it — why this decision, which threats you weighed, what the domain actually means — is the expensive part to reconstruct and the easy part to lose. The Method captures all of it as you go, in plain files in your repo:

Walk into an audit with the evidence already there. Onboard a new hire who can read why, not just what. Come back in two years and still understand your own system. Every decision expertly documented is a return that compounds — and it's yours, independent of whatever model made it.

Stay in control as scope grows

Here's the trap nobody warns you about: the more the AI builds, the less you hold the whole in your head — and the quality that lives across the project, not in any one file, slips without anything turning red. User experience, test adequacy, compliance, NFRs. No diff shows them eroding; a thin test still passes; a missing budget still ships. You stay accountable for them and the least able to see them.

The Method keeps them yours to hold. Inside an epic, the Definition of Ready forces each story to declare its budgets, its migration, its edge cases — no silent gaps. Across the whole project, a stewardship layer rebuilds your view:

/posture

The standing pulse

A quick, recurring snapshot of where UX, testing, compliance, and NFR quality stand — and what's moved since last time. Written to git, so its history is the trend.

/audit

The deep sweep

An on-demand adversarial dig for where a concern has actually drifted — ranked findings with evidence, each pointing back into refinement, where you stay the author.

Now scale it to a team

Here's the problem nobody saw coming: give everyone an AI agent, and alignment falls apart. Each agent makes its own assumptions, re-opens decisions you settled last month, contradicts the architecture two desks over — and forgets all of it the moment the session ends. Multiply that across a team and you get drift: a codebase no two people, and no two agents, understand the same way.

The Method gives your team one shared brain. Its memory layer — gbrain — holds the decisions you've made, the domain's agreed language, the patterns that worked, and every plan you've refined, in a brain your whole team shares. At the start of every session each person's agent pulls from it, so new work is informed by what's already been decided — and checked against it. It even spots the echoes: "this looks structurally like the auth migration you shipped in March — want me to factor that in?" Nobody re-litigates; nobody drifts. The team stays in continual alignment, focused on the right thing at the right time, and it gets sharper the more you ship.

Scoped per project by design — one client's decisions never surface while you're working on another's. The isolation a regulated, multi-client shop needs.

Under the hood

No magic — a panel of ten specialised roles the Method composes around your work: an event-storming facilitator, a scope analyst, an architect, a threat modeller, a test author, an adversarial critic, and more. Each is a thinking partner that pushes back, never a stenographer. You describe what you want in plain language; it picks the depth and the roles, and stops at the spec — your coding tool builds from there.

FAQ

Does it write my production code?

No. The Method stops at a ready spec — stories with failing tests, decisions, threat models, a domain map. Implementation happens in your own coding tool, against everything the Method produced. Separating the two is deliberate: specs are where being wrong is cheap.

How is this different from GitHub Spec Kit or Kiro?

Those generate a spec from your intent — a real step up from prompting blind. The Method is the layer that makes the spec rigorous: it event-storms the domain, captures decisions as ADRs, produces a threat model that counts as real compliance evidence, holds every story to a Definition of Ready, and watches quality drift across the whole project. It's plain markdown in your repo, too — agent-agnostic, no IDE lock-in, yours to tune.

How do I keep quality under control as the AI does more?

That's the problem the Method is built around. Per story, the Definition of Ready drags the non-local concerns — NFRs, migrations, edge cases, compliance — into the open so none slip silently. Across the project, /posture gives you a standing read of where UX, testing, compliance, and NFR quality stand, and /audit digs for where they've drifted. You stay the author of the quality that erodes without anything turning red.

Do I need a tracker?

No. Linear, Jira, and GitHub Issues are supported via MCP, but tracker.type: none keeps everything in git — the tree.yaml becomes the operational state. Without a tracker connected, refinement runs in dry-run mode and still produces all git artifacts.

Which agents does it work with?

It's built on Claude Code (agents + skills), and the artifacts it produces — markdown, YAML, failing tests — are readable by any tool. Cursor and Codex can run the install and build from the specs; session handoffs work across agents too.

Do I have to use it for everything?

No. For a five-line fix, just fix it. The Method is for work where the upstream thinking matters — decisions, designs, threat models, anything bigger than a bug.

What if it gets something wrong?

Push back — the Method assumes you will, and records every pushback. Agents are plain markdown files in your repo: if one keeps making the same mistake, edit .claude/agents/{name}.md and the change applies immediately.
